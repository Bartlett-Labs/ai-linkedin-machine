Most conversations about AI scaling focus on model architecture and training data. Almost nobody talks about the physical infrastructure bottleneck that's quietly building.

Recent polling shows significant public opposition to new data center construction, with surveys ranging from 32% to 65% of Americans opposing builds in their communities. The primary concern isn't abstract — it's electricity prices. And the usual counterargument about local job creation doesn't hold up well when data centers employ relatively few people once they're operational.

This matters for anyone building AI-driven workflows, including in demand planning.

A lot of the automation I build assumes that cloud compute will remain accessible and reasonably priced. That's not a guaranteed future. If regulatory friction and community resistance slow data center expansion, we're looking at potential capacity constraints and cost increases that ripple through every organization running AI workloads.

It's already shaping how I think about design decisions:

- Choosing smaller, more efficient models when they get the job done
- Running preprocessing and feature engineering steps locally where possible
- Being deliberate about what actually needs cloud-scale compute versus what's just defaulting there out of convenience
- Treating compute efficiency as a design constraint, not an afterthought

The era of assuming infinitely scalable, cheap infrastructure might be shorter than we think. Building leaner pipelines isn't just good engineering practice — it might become a competitive necessity.

I wonder how many teams are stress-testing their AI roadmaps against a scenario where compute gets meaningfully more expensive, not less.
