The principles reshaping software engineering are the same ones transforming demand planning.

In software, teams have spent years learning to:

→ Automate repetitive workflows so engineers focus on architecture, not busywork
→ Shift quality checks earlier in the process instead of catching errors at the end
→ Use AI to augment decisions, not replace the people making them

I've been building automation tools for my own demand planning work, and the parallels are striking.

**Automating repetitive workflows.** A huge chunk of a planner's week goes to data pulls, formatting, and reconciliation. That's the equivalent of manual deployments in software. Once you script it, you don't go back.

**Shifting quality checks upstream.** In planning, most forecast errors get caught too late — after the PO is placed, after the container ships. Building validation logic into the data pipeline catches anomalies when there's still time to act.

**AI as augmentation.** The most useful AI tools I've built don't generate forecasts autonomously. They surface patterns I'd miss in a spreadsheet and flag where my assumptions might be off. The judgment still sits with the planner.

What's interesting is that supply chain didn't borrow these ideas from software intentionally. The problems just converge when any complex operation reaches a certain scale: too much manual work, too many handoffs, too little feedback before consequences are locked in.

The tooling differs. The underlying discipline doesn't.

I'm curious whether other operations teams are finding the same convergence in their own workflows.
